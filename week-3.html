Fantasy Shape

Madrid is not a normal clay week. The altitude keeps first-strike tennis live, which means serve bonus hunters matter more here than they do in slower clay events. The tournament also runs standalone from 22 April to 3 May, so fantasy builds should start with players who have realistic semi-final equity, then layer in upside values such as Joao Fonseca and Learner Tien once the draw shows where the pressure points are.

  • Prioritize semi-final equity over one-match upside.
  • Give extra weight to ace production in Madrid conditions.
  • Wait for the draw before stacking one half too aggressively.
  • Watch Barcelona and Munich finalists for turnaround fatigue.

What Matters This Week

ATP Fantasy still gives you a 100-credit budget, six starters, two alternates, and one Bonus Ball player who scores double. The scoring rules do not change, but a standalone ATP Masters 1000 week changes the math because deep advancement is worth more than simply surviving into Thursday.

Player prices are still based on the PIF ATP Live Rankings, which means pre-draw edge usually comes from identifying ranking-driven mispricings before the market settles. Carlos Alcaraz

ATP’s Madrid entry story notes that Alcaraz owns a 15-2 record in Madrid. Home-court history is not everything in fantasy, but that is exactly the sort of event-specific edge that justifies elite pricing.

Casper Ruud

Ruud returns to Madrid as the defending champion after claiming the first ATP Masters 1000 title of his career there in 2025. That matters because Madrid has already proven it can reward his clay patterns over a full two-week run.

Fonseca and Tien

Former Next Gen ATP Finals champions Joao Fonseca and Learner Tien are both in the field. In ranking-based fantasy pricing, that is exactly the profile to watch for underpriced upside.

Bonus Ball Board

1. Carlos Alcaraz

Until the draw says otherwise, Alcaraz is the default Week 3 Bonus Ball. He combines the best Madrid-specific record with home-soil comfort and the strongest pre-draw title case on the board.

2. Jannik Sinner

Sinner’s Masters floor is currently unmatched, and Madrid is another event where his baseline control can erase most of the field. The only real caution is workload if Monte-Carlo goes the distance.

3. Alexander Zverev

Zverev has the kind of serve-plus-first-ball profile that plays well in Madrid altitude, and he already owns a title here. He is the early alternative if you want a high-end anchor outside the obvious Alcaraz/Sinner construction.

Core Plays

Carlos Alcaraz

The best combination of event history, ceiling and likely ownership. If his quarter is not overloaded, he stays central to Week 3 builds.

Jannik Sinner

Even on clay, Sinner is still giving fantasy managers the cleanest elite floor because he wins service games efficiently and rarely leaks points in ugly patches.

Andrey Rublev

Rublev is one of the better ranking-based buy candidates before the draw. Madrid’s conditions suit his first-strike pattern, and former-champion status gives him more real upside here than the market often prices.

Value Targets

Joao Fonseca

Fonseca remains the cleanest upside value archetype in ATP Fantasy. His ranking still trails his actual threat level on big stages, and Madrid’s conditions can amplify his serve-plus-forehand damage.

Learner Tien

Tien is the kind of player who can be mispriced simply because the market is still catching up. If he lands away from the elite traffic, he becomes a real roster consideration.

Ben Shelton

Madrid is one of the few clay stops where Shelton’s ace profile can carry genuine weekly value. If his draw is not packed with elite returners, the scoring format can do the rest.

Alexander Zverev

If ATP Fantasy prices Zverev closer to rank than to Madrid-specific win equity, he will end up in the value conversation even at the high end. Not cheap, but potentially underbought relative to championship odds.

Trap List

Casper Ruud at full defending-champion tax

The Madrid title run is real, but fantasy is about this week’s path and price, not last year’s points. If Ruud is priced aggressively and his draw is crowded, he can become an easy overpay.

Novak Djokovic without draw clarity

Djokovic always carries ceiling, but the pre-draw version of Madrid is exactly where brand-name gravity can outrun actual lineup value. Quarter placement matters too much to lock him blindly.

Anyone arriving heavy from Barcelona or Munich

Back-to-back clay weeks can flip from momentum to fatigue quickly. If a player goes deep in Week 2 and then lands a rough Madrid opener, the risk profile changes immediately.
